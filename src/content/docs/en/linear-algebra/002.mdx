## Elimination

To be able to to forward elimination we need a pivot, to begin the pivot is $\mA_{1,1}$. We need to find a way to make $\mA_{2,1}$ zero, and this can be done using a multiplier and substract the result.

In this example the multiplier is the number $3$.

<Equation>
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 2 & 1 \\
    0 & 2 & -2 \\
    0 & 4 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Forward elimination step 1.
</Equation>

For the pivot $\mA_{2,2}$ we use the multiplier $2$.

<Equation>
    $$
    \mU = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 2 & 1 \\
    0 & 2 & -2 \\
    0 & 0 & 5
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Forward elimination step 2.
</Equation>

The resulting matrix is usually refers as \mU$ for upper triangular.

<Important>
    Pivots **CAN NOT** be zero.
</Important>

WAIT a second, these are equations what happen with the right hand side (rhs)?... We also need to apply the same operations in the same order to the vector $\vb$ (rhs).

<Equation>
    $$
    \vb = \begin{bmatrix}
    2 \\
    12 \\
    2
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Right hand side of the system of linear equations.
</Equation>

After applying the same operations to $\vb$, the following vector is obtained:

<Equation>
    $$
    \vc = \begin{bmatrix}
    2 \\
    6 \\
    -10
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Right hand side of the system of linear equations after applying same operations in $\mA$.
</Equation>

## Back-Substitution

So now with $\mU$ and $\vc$ let's rewrite the system of linear equations:

<Equation>
    $$
    \begin{cases}
    \begin{aligned}
    x + 2y + z &= 2 \\
    2y -2z &= 6 \\
    5z &= -10
    \end{aligned}
    \end{cases}
    $$
    System of linear equations after elimination.
</Equation>

How do we solve this for $x, y, z$ ?... simple right ?

<Equation>
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    x &= 2 \\
    y &= 1 \\
    z &= -2
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    Solution for our system of linear equations.
</Equation>

This process id called **back-substitution**.

## Elimination Matrices

Wouldn't it be cool if we could put the manual operations we did earlier into a matrix ?

<Equation>
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 0 & 0 \\
    -3 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 2 & 1 \\
    3 & 8 & 1 \\
    0 & 4 & 1
    \end{bmatrix} = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 2 & 1 \\
    0 & 2 & -2 \\
    0 & 4 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Step 1 of the forward elimination using elimination matrices.
</Equation>

Let's call this elimination/elementary matrix $\mE_{2,1}$.
<Equation>
    $$
    \mE_{2,1} = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 0 & 0 \\
    -3 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Elimination matrix $\mE_{2,1}$.
</Equation>

<Equation>
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & -2 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 2 & 1 \\
    0 & 2 & -2 \\
    0 & 4 & 1
    \end{bmatrix} = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 2 & 1 \\
    0 & 2 & -2 \\
    0 & 0 & 5
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Step 2 of the forward elimination.
</Equation>

<Equation>
    $$
    \mE_{3,2} = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & 1 & 0 \\
    0 & -2 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Elimination matrix $\mE_{3,2}$.
</Equation>

Means that we can write the operations as follows:

<Equation>
    $$
    \mE_{3,2} (\mE_{2,1} \mA) = \mU
    $$
    Using matrix operations for matrices elimination.
</Equation>

We can change the paranthesis:

<Equation>
    $$
    (\mE_{3,2} \mE_{2,1}) \mA = \mU
    $$
    By the associative law of matrices.
</Equation>


<Important>
    You **CANT NOT** change the order.
</Important>

## Permutation Matrices

If we have the matrix:

<Equation>
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix}
    a & b \\
    c & d
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Example matrix for permutation.
</Equation>

What matrix can we use to exchange **row 1** with **row 2**?

<Equation>
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix}
    0 & 1 \\
    1 & 0
    \end{bmatrix}
    \begin{bmatrix}
    a & b \\
    c & d
    \end{bmatrix} = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
    c & d \\
    a & b
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Example of permutation operation.
</Equation>

Suppose we want to do columns instead... How can we do that?

<Equation>
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix}
    a & b \\
    c & d
    \end{bmatrix}
    \begin{bmatrix}
    0 & 1 \\
    1 & 0
    \end{bmatrix} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
    b & a \\
    d & c
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$
    Example of permutation operation.
</Equation>

Can you notice a pattern ?... Yep.
- For **row** operations the matrix goes to the **left**. 
- For **column** operations the matrix goes to the **right**
