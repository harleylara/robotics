### Way 1 Matrix Multiplication: Dot product

<Equation
formula="(\mA \mB)\mC = \mA (\mB \mC)"
description="Fundamental Law of Matrix Multiplication"
/>

1. Matrices $\mA$ with $n$ **columns** multiply matrices $\mB$ with $n$ **rows**

<Equation
formula="\mA_{m \times n} \mB_{n \times p} = \mC_{m \times p}"
/>
<Important
    note="To multiply $\mA \mB$: if $\mA$ has $n$ **columns**, $\mB$ MUST have $n$ **rows**"
/>

<Drawio 
    file="linear-algebra-003.drawio"
    page="1"
    description="Matrix multiplication as a set of dot product operations."
/>

From [wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product): In mathematics, the dot product or scalar product is an algebraic operation that takes two equal-length sequences of numbers (usually coordinate vectors), and returns a single number.

- If $\mA$ and $\mB$ are $n$ by $n$, this operation involve $n^2$ dot products.
- Each dot product needs $n$ **multiplications**, So the multiplication of $\mA \mB$ uses $n^2 n = n^3$ multiplications!
- Each dot product needs $n-1$ **additions**, So in total $n^2(n-1) = n^3 - n^2$ are required.

**Hint**: In matrix multiplication every column of $\mB$ is multiplied by $\mA$.

2. Each entry in $\mA \mB  = \mC$ is a **dot product**: $\mC_{ij}=(\textrm{row} i \textrm{of} \mA) \dot (\textrm{column} j \textrm{of} \mB)$

<Equation
    formula="\begin{bmatrix}
    0 \\
    1 \\
    2
    \end{bmatrix}
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 2 & 3
    \end{bmatrix} = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
    0 & 0 & 0 \\
    1 & 2 & 3 \\
    2 & 4 & 6
    \end{bmatrix}"
    description="A row times a column is an *'inner'* product-that is another name for dot product. A column times a row is an *'outer'* product."
/>

<Important
    note="It is not usually true that $\mA \mB = \mB \mA$. In most cases $\mA$ doesn't commute with $\mB$."
/>

## The Laws for Matrix Operations

Commutative law:

<Equation
    formula="\mA + \mB = B + A"
    description="Matrix addition is commutative."
/>

Distributive law:

<Equation
    formula="c(\mA + \mB) = c \mA + c \mB"
    description="Distributive law."
/>

Assosiative law:

<Equation
    formula="\mA + (\mB + \mC) = (\mA + \mB) + \mC"
    description="Assosiative law."
/>

<Equation
    formula="\mA \mB \neq \mB \mA"
    description="Commutative 'law' is usually broken"
/>

<Equation
    formula="\mA (\mB + \mC) = \mA \mB + \mA \mC"
    description="Distributive law from the left."
/>

<Equation
    formula="(\mA + \mB)\mC = \mA \mC + \mB \mC"
    description="Distributive law from the right."
/>

<Equation
    formula="\mA(\mB \mC) = (\mA \mB)\mC"
    description="Distributive law from the right."
/>

## Inverse Matrices

Let's take a square matrix $\mA$.

1. **IF** the square matrix $\mA$ has an inverse:

<Equation
    formula="\mA^{-1} \mA = I \\ \mA \mA^{-1} = I"
    description="associative law for $\mA \mB \mC$"
/>

These cool matrices are:
- Invertible
- Non-singular

### Singular Case

<Equation
    formula="\mA = \begin{bmatrix}
    1 & 3 \\
    2 & 6
    \end{bmatrix}"
/>

Can we find a matrix $\mD$ that when multiplied with $\mA$ will give us the identity matrix?

### Inverse of a Product $\mA \mB$

If $\mA$ and $\mB$ are invertible then so is $\mA \mB$. The inverse of a product $\mA \mB$ is:

<Equation
    formula="(\mA \mB)^{-1}= \mB^{-1} \mA^{-1}"
    description="Inverse of a product"
/>

Yeap, is in reverse here is why.

We assume that:

<Equation
    formula="(\mA \mB) = (\mB^{-1} \mA^{-1})"
    description=""
/>

So if we move the inverses to the left hand side (lhs) this should be equal to the identity matrix:

<Equation
    formula="(\mA \mB) (\mB^{-1} \mA^{-1}) = \mI
    \mA \mB \mB^{-1} \mA^{-1} = \mA \mI \mA^{-1} = \mA \mA^{-1} = \mI"
    description=""
/>

Keep in mind:

<Equation
    formula="(\mA \mB \mC)^{-1} = \mC^{-1} \mB^{-1} \mA^{-1}"
    description=""
/>
